Wind River review

 

·        Set in a snowy Indian reservation

·        An animal tracker teams up with an FBI agent to catch the murderer of a young Native American girl

·        The writer of Hell or High water and Sicario

·        Starring Jeremy Renner and Elizabeth Olsen

 

At the time of writing this, Wind River is my front runner for best movie of the year. The acting was outstanding on all accounts, in fact, Jeremy Renner and Elizabeth Olsen work so well together on screen it makes me long for a Hawk Eye/ Scarlett Witch spinoff. The writer/director Taylor Sheridan doesn’t tell wild extravagant stories and he doesn’t embellish in the name of being cinematic. He finds ways of life, and the simple truths hidden in the cracks within and lets them speak for themselves. And holy hell, do they speak!

Carving out his story using a tool I haven’t seen much of in the last decade: patience - Sheridan is able to immerse us in this unambiguous, barebones “luck don’t live here” as Jeremy Renner’s character puts it, world. The writer’s true talent is in capturing culture and circumstance and letting them unfold on their own until the bitter end.

Like the last couple Sheridan has penned, this has the feel of a modern day western. Only unlike most westerns, modern or not, the antagonist is more than just an evil man who can be eliminated, restoring peace and happiness to everything around, it’s also a series of systemic shortcomings that live within us and all around us. In setting this story on a snowy desolate Indian reservation he strips away the modern comforts that distract and occupy, giving us a portrait of how dark it can get when you have nothing to entertain your mind but painful memories and regrets. This film captures generations of suffering and mistakes and leaves you feeling affected long after the ending credits.

Now’s as good of a time as any, I suppose, to mention that the movie is also really funny at times. For as dark and hopeless feeling as the subject matter is, especially considering it’s inspired by a real-life epidemic (the film ends with the haunting words “while missing person statistics are compiled for every other demographic, none exist for Native American women.”), the film never feels like a Sarah McLachlan commercial. That is to say, it’s by no means all medicine. you don’t have to be in any particular mood to appreciate this one, though maybe pick a bubblier one for a first date.

PRESCRIPTION:

Double feature: Watch Hell or High Water and Wind River back to back and enjoy a night of forgetting about yourself.
