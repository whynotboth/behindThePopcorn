The Foreigner Review 2017

 

·        After his daughter is killed in a terrorist attack, a restaurant owner looking for vengeance squares off with a government official; both of whom have dangerous pasts.

·        Starring Jackie Chan and Pierce Brosnan.

·        Written by David Maroni: Enemy of the state, Live Free or Die Hard, Collision.

·        Directed by Martin Campbell: Goldeneye, The Mask of Zorro, Casino Royale.

·        Based on the book The Chinaman by Stephen Leather.

 

 

The Foreigner is sort of two different movies. On the one hand you have a story about Jackie Chan going on a one-way mission of retribution, but on the other, you have a political drama where Pierce Brosnan tries to navigate the destabilizing harmony between his cultural loyalties and political responsibilities.

As intriguing as both are (and as masterfully told as I’m sure it was in the book), the movie would have benefited from cutting out one and focusing on the other more fully. I respect the craft involved in showing every character’s unique plight, but in a father-wants-revenge movie, the ride goes a lot smoother when you’re not forced to empathize with the bad guy, and the good guy isn’t afraid to inflict more than a walk-in visits worth of damage to the people in his way.

By telling the story from both sides, we weren’t treated to the tunnel vision feeling of reckless abandon and all-consuming certainty that punctuate the genre. Instead, we were served a birds-eye view of the painful shades of grey that, more realistically, riddle these sorts of conflicts.

While I appreciated being privy to the whole picture: Ignorance, as they say, is bliss. I prefer my bloodlust movies to be belligerently short sited. Me good, you bad. *knocks out henchman’s teeth with something inventive*

Hopefully this isn’t saying more about me than the movie but, the inciting murder didn’t really phase me. They didn’t let us get to know the daughter well enough to care that she died, and Chan’s character was far too reasonable and polite to stir up any real tribal hate. What I found affecting me the most was the smaller interrelationship betrayals and deceits that took place behind closed doors.

Jackie Chan and Pierce Brosnan were both fantastic and while seeing the pair tomcat around on the big screen was reason enough to justify my movie ticket, If you don’t hold the actors in the same regard as I, you can probably hold off on this one.

 

PRESCRIPTION:

To see Brosnan play a similar role more fully: The Ghost writer.

To see a better revenge style movie: Watch Taken, or basically any movie Mel Gibson made in the 90’s.
